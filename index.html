Introductions

Esm-e man Mohammad ast  /esme mæn mohæmmæd æst/ My name is Muhammad / I am Muhammad
Formal written: است (ast). Standard spoken: هستم (hastam). Colloquial: "to be" fused to noun (محمدم)
اسم شما چیست؟
Esm-e shumā chist? / Esmet chiye?  /esme ʃomɒː t͡ʃiːst/ What is your name?
Formal/Dari: اسم شما چیست؟ — Colloquial Iranian: اسمت چیه؟

ممنون

Stories

خواب بد — A Bad Dream
شب بود
Shab būd. It was night.
من در بیت بودم و خواب بودم
Man dar bayt būdam va khwāb būdam. I was at home and was asleep.
در خواب یک اژدها بود
Dar khwāb yek izhdihā būd. In the dream, there was a dragon.
اژدها سیاه بود و خیلی بد بود
Izhdihā siyāh būd va kheylī bad būd. The dragon was black and was very bad.
صدای اژدها شدید بود
Ṣadā-ye izhdihā shadīd būd. The sound of the dragon was intense.
من گفتم وای بس است
Man goftam: vāy! Bas ast! I said: oh my! That is enough!
بعد صبح بود
Ba'd ṣubḥ būd. Then it was morning.
خواب بد بود ولی صبح تازه بود
Khwāb bad būd valī ṣubḥ tāze būd. The dream was bad, but the morning was fresh.
بوی سبزه خوش بود
Bū-ye sabze khush būd. The smell of herbs was pleasant.
خدا را شکر
Khudā rā shukr! Thank God!

توپ محمد — Mohammad's Ball
محمد در بیت بود
Moḥammad dar bayt būd. Mohammad was at home.
یک توپ بود
Yek tūp būd. There was a ball.
توپ از در رفت
Tūp az dar raft. The ball went through the door.
محمد گفت وای توپ من
Moḥammad goft: vāy! Tūp-e man! Mohammad said: oh my! My ball!
محمد رفت ولی جوراب او پاره بود
Moḥammad raft valī jūrāb-e ū pāra būd. Mohammad went, but his sock was torn.
یک شخص در شهر گفت جوراب شما پاره است
Yek shakhs dar shahr goft: jūrāb-e shumā pāra ast! A person in the city said: your sock is torn!
محمد گفت بس توپ من
Moḥammad goft: bas! Tūp-e man! Mohammad said: enough! My ball!
شخص گفت توپ شما سبز بود
Shakhs goft: tūp-e shumā sabz būd? The person said: was your ball green?
محمد گفت نه سیاه بود
Moḥammad goft: na, siyāh būd. Mohammad said: no, it was black.
بعد توپ پیدا بود
Ba'd tūp paydā būd. Then the ball was found.
محمد خوش بود ولی جوراب هم پاره بود
Moḥammad khush būd valī jūrāb ham pāra būd! Mohammad was happy, but the sock was also torn!
بی‌چاره محمد
Be-chāra Moḥammad! Poor Mohammad!

تاج شاه — The King's Crown
یک شاه بود
Yek shāh būd. There was a king.
تاج او زر بود
Tāj-e ū zar būd. His crown was gold.
سرای او خاص بود
Sarāy-e ū khāṣ būd. His palace was special.
ولی شاه خوش نبود
Valī shāh khush nabūd. But the king was not happy.
او گفت چرا احساس من بد است
Ū goft: chirā iḥsās-e man bad ast? He said: why is my feeling bad?
تاج زر هست ولی احساس خوب نیست
Tāj-e zar hast valī iḥsās-e khūb nīst. There is a gold crown, but the feeling is not good.
یک شخص از دور آمد
Yek shakhs az dūr āmad. A person came from far.
شخص گفت صبر
Shakhs goft: ṣabr! The person said: patience!
زر بد نیست ولی سخاوت خاص است
Zar bad nīst valī sakhāvat khāṣ ast. Gold is not bad, but generosity is special.
شاه آب داد
Shāh āb dād. The king gave water.
چای هم داد و قند هم داد
Chāy ham dād va qand ham dād. He also gave tea and also gave sugar cubes.
بعد احساس شاه خوب بود
Ba'd iḥsās-e shāh khūb būd. Then the king's feeling was good.
شاه گفت جدّی سخاوت خاص است
Shāh goft: jiddī! Sakhāvat khāṣ ast! The king said: truly! Generosity is special!

Grammar & Cultural Notes

Grammar
"To be" verb (است / هست)
Three registers: Formal written: است (ast) — e.g., اسم من محمد است. Standard spoken: هستم (hastam). Colloquial: "to be" fused to noun — e.g., من محمدم (man muḥammadam). Negation: نیستم (nīstam). NPI2
Ezafe (-e after consonants, -ye after vowels)
Linking particle connecting nouns to modifiers; pronounced but often not written. E.g., گویش ایرانی (gūyish-e īrānī) = "the Iranian dialect"; اسم شما (esm-e shumā) = "your name"; خانه‌ی من (khāne-ye man) = "my house." NPI2, NPI3
Pluralization with -hā
The suffix ها (-hā) forms plurals. E.g., کتاب → کتابها (ketāb → ketāb-hā). NPI5
می (me-/mi-) prefix
Present-tense marker before verbs. E.g., عذر می‌خواهم ('uzr me-khāham) = "I seek pardon." NPI5
Tashdīd (consonant doubling)
The ـّ mark doubles a consonant. Correctly doubled: Muhammad (مُحَمَّد), Abbas, Muharram. Incorrectly doubled in English: Assad, Hussain, Issa — no tashdīd in original. NPI2
Non-connector letters
These only connect to the preceding letter: ا (Alef), د (Dāl), ر (Rā'), ذ (Zāl), ز (Ze), ژ (Zhe), و (Vāv). Only two positional forms instead of four. NPI2, NPI4, NPI5

Cultural Notes
  • او (Ū) = He / She / They — Persian is gender-neutral in the third-person pronoun. NPI1
  • شیر (shīr) homograph: means Lion, Milk, or Faucet depending on context — same script, different meanings. Illustrates why context matters more than vowel marks in Persian. NPI1
  • بابا آب داد ("Dad gave water") is the iconic first sentence in Iranian elementary school reading primers, equivalent to "See Spot run." NPI2
  • Persian language names: پارسی (Pārsī) in Iran, دری (Darī) in Afghanistan, تاجیکی (Tājīkī) in Tajikistan (Cyrillic script). NPI3
  • تاج (Tāj, "Crown") is the root of تاجیکی (Tājīkī) — etymological link to the Tajik people. NPI3
  • سبزه (Sabze) refers to sprouted herbs placed on the Haft-Sin table for Nowruz. NPI4
  • سیزده بدر (Sīzdah-be-dar): Nowruz tradition on the 13th day — going outdoors. Literally "thirteen-out-the-door." NPI4
  • بحر vs. دریا: Both mean "sea" — بحر (Baḥr) is Arabic-origin, دریا (Daryā) is Persian-origin. NPI4
  • نه صورت، نه سیرت (na ṣūrat, na sīrat): Insult meaning "neither good appearance nor good character." NPI5
  • Letter shape families: ب/پ/ت/ث share a base shape. ج/چ/ح/خ share a base shape. ر/ز/ژ share a base shape. س/ش share a three-toothed base. ص/ض share a looped base. Dot patterns are key. NPI2, NPI3, NPI5, NPI6
  • Persian tea culture: Tea (چای) is the national drink. A teapot of strong-steeped tea (چای دمی) sits atop a سماور (samovar) of boiling water. Drinkers request کم‌رنگ (light) or پررنگ (dark). Tea is served in a tulip-shaped استکان (istikan) on a نعلبکی (saucer), sweetened by holding a قند (sugar cube) or نبات (rock candy) between the teeth while sipping — چای قند پهلو. NPI6
  • Silent و in خوش (khush, "good/nice") — the و is written but not pronounced. NPI6
